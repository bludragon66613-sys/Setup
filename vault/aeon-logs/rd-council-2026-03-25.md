## Top 3 Recommendations

### 1. Build an AI-Powered Solar EPC Management SaaS for the Indian Market
**Why:** India has 6,261 solar startups and thousands of EPC installers, but only ~250 Energy SaaS companies serve them. Most small installers in Tier-2/3 cities run on WhatsApp and spreadsheets. Tata Power just partnered with Salesforce for their enterprise version, proving the market wants this — but the SMB installer segment (the vast majority) is completely underserved. Rohan's agent-building expertise (NERV, Aeon, 156 agents) maps directly onto this problem.
**Do this:** Validate demand by interviewing 10 solar EPC installers in Tier-2 Indian cities (Jaipur, Lucknow, Coimbatore) about their pain points in lead management, subsidy paperwork, and project tracking. Use Aeon to automate outreach and scheduling.
**Timeline:** 2-3 weeks for validation; 6-8 weeks for MVP
**Aeon can help:** Yes — build a research skill that scrapes PM Surya Ghar installer directories, collects contact info, and auto-generates interview outreach. Aeon can also monitor MNRE policy changes daily.

### 2. Launch a Solar Subsidy Navigator / Lead-Gen Agent
**Why:** The PM Surya Ghar scheme offers up to INR 78,000 in subsidies per household, but the application process is confusing for homeowners. Only 16.51 lakh of the targeted 1 crore households have enrolled — a 98.3% untapped market. An AI chatbot/agent that walks homeowners through eligibility, calculates savings, and connects them with local installers could capture massive lead-gen revenue (INR 500-2,000 per qualified lead to installers). Solar Capital just raised funds doing something similar but without AI agents.
**Do this:** Build a WhatsApp-based chatbot (using Claude API) that asks 5 questions, checks PM Surya Ghar eligibility, estimates savings based on electricity bill, and connects to a local installer. Deploy in one city first.
**Timeline:** 3-4 weeks for working prototype
**Aeon can help:** Yes — Aeon can power the backend intelligence: track subsidy policy changes, maintain installer databases, and generate daily market reports. The claude-api skill can scaffold the chatbot.

### 3. Create an India Green Energy Intelligence Newsletter/Platform
**Why:** India's carbon credit trading scheme (CCTS) launches mid-2026, covering 700M tonnes of CO2 across 9 sectors. Open access solar for C&I is booming. Module prices are up 10-15% since December. The market is complex and fast-moving — corporate energy buyers, investors, and small developers all need synthesized intelligence. Rohan already has the content-generation infrastructure (Aeon's digest skills, article skill, RSS/HN pipelines) to produce this at near-zero marginal cost.
**Do this:** Launch a weekly "India Green Energy Brief" using Aeon's existing digest infrastructure. Publish 4 editions, measure engagement, then decide whether to productize into a paid Substack or embed into the SaaS platform (Rec #1).
**Timeline:** 1 week to first edition
**Aeon can help:** Yes — compose a new skill from rss-digest + web-search + article skills. Auto-publish weekly. Aeon already has the pipeline; it just needs solar-specific RSS feeds and search queries configured.

## Council Dissent

The Devil's Advocate raised a strong objection to Recommendation #1 (Solar EPC SaaS): **Rohan has no existing presence in the Indian solar market, no domain relationships, and no distribution channel.** Building SaaS for an industry you're outside of is the #1 startup failure mode. The counterargument (from The Builder) is that the validation phase is cheap (2-3 weeks of interviews) and Rohan's agent-building skills create a genuine technical moat — but the risk is real. If the 10 installer interviews don't reveal a clear, painful, *paying* problem, pivot to Rec #3 (intelligence platform) which requires zero industry relationships to start.

The Devil's Advocate also noted: **India's solar market has execution risk at scale** — 42 GW of awarded renewable capacity has no power supply contracts, and the government is slowing tender pace until FY2027. This doesn't kill the opportunity but means timing matters: focus on the rooftop/distributed segment (PM Surya Ghar, C&I open access) rather than utility-scale, where policy uncertainty is highest.

### The Devil's Advocate

**Challenge 1: "Build a Solar EPC SaaS" assumes installers will pay for software — most won't**
- *Claim:* The Analyst says 6,000+ installers are underdigitized and need SaaS tooling.
- *Counterpoint:* Most Indian solar installers are small businesses with 5-15 employees, razor-thin margins (15-25% on residential), and zero software budget. Solar Ladder already offers its platform for FREE and still struggles with adoption. The Indian SMB SaaS market is notoriously hard — churn is high, willingness to pay is low, and sales cycles are long. Tata Power can afford Salesforce; a 10-person installer in Jaipur cannot.
- *What to watch for:* During the 10 installer interviews, specifically ask: "What do you currently pay for software?" and "Would you pay INR 2,000/month for a tool that saves you 10 hours/week?" If more than 7/10 say no, pivot immediately.

**Challenge 2: The subsidy navigator idea sounds good but has a razor-thin defensibility window**
- *Claim:* The Futurist says PM Surya Ghar has 98.3% untapped market for a lead-gen agent.
- *Counterpoint:* Subsidy navigators are trivially copyable. The moment it works, every solar installer and fintech will build one. The government itself will likely improve the application UX. WhatsApp chatbots for lead gen are a commodity. The only moat is speed-to-market and installer network quality — both of which Rohan currently has zero of.
- *What to watch for:* If building Rec #2, plan for commoditization from day one. The chatbot is the hook, not the product. The product must be the installer network and the data layer (which installers are reliable, which areas convert best, what's the average ticket size by city).

**Challenge 3: "India will produce 3-5 solar rooftop unicorns" ignores that most funded solar startups are burning cash**
- *Claim:* The Futurist points to Aerem ($19.2M), Freyr ($11.6M), MYSUN ($9M) as proof of VC interest.
- *Counterpoint:* Funded ≠ profitable. Many Indian solar startups are subsidized by VC money and government incentives. When either dries up, unit economics collapse. The 42 GW of awarded capacity without power contracts is a warning sign. Module prices rising 10-15% squeezes installer margins further. Entering now means competing with funded players who can underprice you indefinitely.
- *What to watch for:* Module price trajectory over the next 90 days (China VAT rebate withdrawal on April 1 will be the test). If prices spike >20%, small installers will freeze — and so will demand for their tools.
